Project Brief

Thoughts on where our project is heading

Traditional Navigation systems often fail to guide pedestrians. Places like plazas, building interiors, subway stops, and places without addresses (e.g. mall interiors) are not navigable with gps based navigation systems. Many of these spaces lack proper signage and can be very confusing to foot traffic. Here are some examples of spaces that fall within this space.

  • Museums are densely packed with a large number of exhibits that can change rapidly, and the paper leaflet/wall maps often do a poor job of directing you if you want to find a specific installation.
  • Plazas and other large open spaces without paths or roads are spaces that traditional navigation apps have trouble with (usual direction is “Head east on Speen St”, but which was is Speen St, and which way is east?)
  • Subway stops are underground, and occasionally have trouble directing you as to which train to get on and which direction to exit the station to head in the direction you want to go?
  • Malls occasionally have competent internal signage (there have been an increased number of electronic map stations, but there usually aren’t enough of them and their instructions aren’t portable), but it’s often difficult to know where to enter the mall/which floor to be on if you have a specific store you want to go to.
  • Large colleges (like Wellesley) have many different buildings with many floors. They sometimes contain older buildings were very oddly numbered rooms and twisting hallways. It is hard to find any specific room if you are not familiar with campus. It is also hard to find the unlocked doors after normal business hours.
  • Conventions / Conferences: While the overall layout of the convention center does not change, many different conferences and conventions things like grandstands and rows of booths. This can make it hard to navigate the space even if you have been there before.
  • Hospitals: Many hospitals contain specialist offices nestled within the building. Many times, the hallways all look nearly identical and it is difficult to find the correct office.

How we will change the status quo

This will change the status quo for pedestrians, mass transit users, and building navigators. It will make it easier to navigate new spaces and will lower the stress of walking somewhere new. It helps a wide variety of people especially those who are new to an area or are directionally challenged.

First Sketch

One option we are considering would be a maps-type navigation, but that works indoors, likely using image processing and dead reckoning to guide you to your location. It might also involve showing a blue-print type overview of the area you are in.

What we need

We need to determine how people best get around on foot, and what spaces they have trouble navigating. We’d also want to discuss how they’d like to be guided through those spaces, and how they’d want those directions delivered. We’d also want to figure out how they’ve navigated such spaces in the past. Our primary method of data collection would be interviews with our target users. Reasonable groups we could contact and interview include:

  • Cross registered/new students on college campuses - particularly larger campuses like MIT, or campuses with confusing internal layouts, like Wellesley
  • Students who have done internships in new cities, with unfamiliar transit systems, to reach and enter unfamiliar buildings
  • Frequent fliers - people who regularly visit new and unfamiliar airports and would like a better way to find their way around them
  • People who visit lots of libraries looking for specific things -- or talk to librarians about what people constantly ask them about in terms of directions
  • People who shop in department stores, often with a specific product in mind
  • Inspirational Conversations: retail store employees who have to direct shoppers

To accomplish this effectively, we will have weekly meetings and communicate effectively through group chats and bi-weekly plus/delta sessions. We will voice concerns when we have them and will strive to keep the learning goals of others in mind when making decisions.
